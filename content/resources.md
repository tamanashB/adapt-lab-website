## Lab Philosophy

"Somewhere, something incredible is waiting to be known." – Carl Sagan

In our lab, we believe that pushing the boundaries of knowledge requires bold inquiry. We are committed to building a vibrant lab culture that is inclusive, collaborative, and supportive. We believe that good science requires a safe and supportive environment where everyone feels comfortable asking questions, making mistakes, and sharing ideas without fear of judgment.

### Expectations

**Everyone in the Lab**
- Treat all lab members with respect and kindness
- Be honest and rigorous in your science
- Take responsibility of your research
- Communicate openly and early if something isn't working, scientifically or personally
- Take care of shared spaces and equipments
- Attend and contribute to lab meetings
- Respect each other's time and personal boundaries
- Celebrate each other's successes

**The PI's Commitment to You**
- Provide mentorship tailored to your career goals
- Give timely feedback on manuscripts, proposals, and presentations
- Advocate for your success inside and outside the lab
- Maintain an open door policy
- Respect your time and personal boundaries

### Working Hours

We trust lab members to manage their time effectively and meet their scientific goals. That said, we expect everyone to be present for scheduled lab meetings, seminars, and collaborative commitments. Science is a marathon, not a sprint. Therefore, sustainable work habits are encouraged.

### Meetings

- **Lab meetings** — weekly, all lab members present their work on a rotating basis
- **One-on-ones** — regular individual meetings with the PI to discuss progress, challenges, and career development
- **Journal club** — rotating discussion of recent papers relevant to the lab's interests

### Authorship and Credit

We follow the guidelines of the National Institutes of Health for authorship. Contributions to a project will be discussed openly and early. We are committed to giving appropriate credit to everyone involved in a project.

### Data and Code

- All raw data must be stored and backed up according to lab protocols
- Code used in publications must be deposited in a public repository (e.g. GitHub)
- Lab notebooks (physical or electronic) must be kept up to date

### Mental Health and Wellbeing

Graduate school and postdoctoral training can be stressful. Please never hesitate to reach out to the PI or a trusted colleague if you are struggling. Your wellbeing is a priority. WashU offers a range of mental health resources including:

- [WashU Student Health Services](https://studenthealth.med.wustl.edu/primary-care-resources/mental-health-information/)
- [WashU Center for Counseling & Psychological Services](https://counseling.washu.edu/)

## Recommended Reading

### Scientific Thinking and Reasoning

**Strong Inference** — John R. Platt, *Science*, 1964. A classic essay arguing that the most productive scientists systematically apply alternative hypothesis testing. Required reading for every new lab member.

**Strong Inference for Systems Biology** — Beard & Kushmerick, *PLOS Computational Biology*, 2009. An update of Platt's framework applied to modern systems biology.

### Scientific Writing

**Ten Simple Rules for Structuring Papers** — Mensh & Kording, *PLOS Computational Biology*, 2017. Practical, concise guide to writing papers that are clear and compelling. Free and open access.

### Career Development and Lab Life

**Making the Right Moves** — Burroughs Wellcome Fund & HHMI. A comprehensive free guide covering lab leadership, mentoring, time management, getting funded, and navigating academic careers. Essential reading for postdocs and new faculty.

### Books Worth Reading

- **A PhD Is Not Enough** — Peter Feibelman. Practical career advice for scientists, covering everything from job talks to navigating academic politics.
- **Writing Science** — Joshua Schimel. The best book on how to tell a scientific story clearly and compellingly.
- **How to Write a Lot** — Paul Silvia. Short, funny, and genuinely useful guide to building consistent writing habits.
- **The Craft of Research** — Booth, Colomb & Williams. Excellent on structuring arguments and writing with clarity.
- **At the Helm** — Kathy Barker. Practical lab management guide covering everything from hiring to data management.

### Virology and Evolutionary Thinking

- **The Logic of Chance** — Eugene Koonin. A deep and accessible exploration of evolutionary thinking in virology, covering the origins and evolution of viruses and life itself. Highly recommended for anyone in the lab.
- **Virolution** — Frank Ryan. Explores how viruses have shaped the evolution of life, including the role of endogenous retroviruses in the human genome.
- **Spillover** — David Quammen. A compelling narrative account of zoonotic viruses and how they jump from animals to humans. Accessible and scientifically grounded — great for understanding the broader context of our work.
- **The Coming Plague** — Laurie Garrett. A classic on emerging infectious diseases and the ecological and evolutionary pressures that drive them.
- **Virus: An Illustrated Guide to 101 Incredible Microbes** — Marilyn Roossinck. A beautifully illustrated introduction to viral diversity — good for reminding yourself how remarkable these entities are.

### Textbooks and Reference Works

- **Fields Virology** — Knipe, Howley et al. The definitive reference in virology. Dense but comprehensive — use it to look up anything about a specific virus family, replication strategy, or pathogenesis mechanism.
- **Principles of Virology** — Flint, Racaniello, Skalka & colleagues. Excellent on molecular mechanisms of viral replication. Highly recommended for graduate students early in their training.
- **Molecular Biology of the Cell** — Alberts et al. The gold standard cell biology reference. Essential background for understanding host-virus interactions at the molecular level.
- **Molecular Biology of the Gene** — Watson et al. Classic reference for molecular biology fundamentals — gene expression, DNA replication, and the central dogma. Good grounding for anyone who needs to strengthen their molecular biology foundation.
- **Evolution** — Futuyma & Kirkpatrick. The standard evolutionary biology textbook. Useful for understanding the evolutionary frameworks we apply to viral systems.
